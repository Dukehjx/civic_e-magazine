Who Owns the Sky? Building a Shared Low-Altitude Economy in the Yangtze River Delta

关于打造长三角海上低空经济示范区的建议

低空经济是新质生产力的典型代表，具有空间立体性、产业融合性及辐射带动性等特征。海洋是发展低空经济的重要场景之一，在海上物流配送、海洋牧场建设、海洋能源运维、海洋文旅观光等诸多领域，低空经济都有广泛的应用前景。长三角拥有江、湾、岛、海多元化的陆海地理空间资源，为发展海上低空经济提供了天然优势。目前，长三角三省一市均已出台专项的低空经济发展规划方案，考虑到长三角沿海城市在资源禀赋、基础设施及产业条件等方面的协同互补性，长三角沿海城市有条件也有必要合力共建海上低空经济示范区，这既可以避免重复建设和资源浪费，也能够促进优势互补，让低空经济成为推动长三角一体化发展的重要抓手。

一、长三角共建海上低空经济示范区的可行性

（一）长三角共建海上低空经济示范区高度契合国家战略

一方面，契合长三角一体化发展战略的决策部署。《长江三角洲区域一体化发展三年行动计划（2024—2026年）》明确提出建设区域低空基础设施网络。低空经济作为跨区域协同的新型载体，能够串联上海港、宁波舟山港、南通港等世界级港口群节点，助力形成“陆海空”立体联动的沿海经济带。另一方面，契合长三角沿海“先飞区”的差异化试点定位。上海提出建设“海-岸-城”智慧物流商业体系，南通提出布局江海特色低空产业，宁波提出打造濒海临湾特色的低空生态领跑城市，舟山提出打造低空经济海岛发展样板。各地均把“低空+海洋”作为应用场景培育的重心又具有一定差异性。共建海上低空经济示范区有助于各地因地制宜合力做大低空经济“蛋糕”。

（二）长三角共建海上低空经济示范区具备区位协同条件

一是地理资源互补。上海临港、江苏启东拥有江海交汇的独特区位，浙江舟山、宁波密布深水港湾与海岛群，江苏东台、浙江台州具备广阔海湾资源，多元地貌构成“江—湾—岛—海”的立体空间，为低空飞行提供全方位场景支撑。二是空域连片协同。目前全国通航使用低空空域不足30%且未成网连片，而长三角低空经济已经实现局部区域的连片发展，如沪舟甬低空航线已实现跨省域海岛覆盖。未来长三角沿海城市进一步的“抱团”发展将有助于推动空域从“点状开放”向“连片贯通”升级。三是陆海产业联动。依托沪宁杭高速、沿海高铁等陆上干线，联动长三角世界级港口群，统筹上海青浦、浙江德清、江苏启东的通用机场与舟山、台州无人机起降场，可构建“陆地基地—港口枢纽—海上拓展”的立体网络。

（三）长三角共建海上低空经济示范区具备经济联动空间

一是产业链条互补协同。上海集聚全国50%的eVTOL创新企业，可主导研发与标准制定；江苏南京浦口、东台等地集聚50余家低空制造企业，形成无人机整机与零部件配套能力；浙江舟山拥有华东最大试飞空域。长三角沿海城市可协同打造“研发—制造—试飞—运营”的全产业链闭环。二是应用场景存在较大公约数。长三角沿海城市在海洋牧场监测、海上风电运维和海岛物流配送等海上低空领域面临相似需求，合力共建的低空平台可在多个场景中同步推广，形成批量化运行模式并摊薄运营成本。三是资源共享共用，避免重复建设。宁波在通用机场方面具备先发优势，前湾新区、宁海通用航空机场建设已投入建设；舟山则在无人机起降场建设方面，已谋划布局建设23个无人机起降场。通过统筹规划通用机场、无人机起降点和通信网络，两地可有效减少投资成本、避免闲置浪费。四是基建共享空间广阔。通过统筹规划通用机场、无人机起降点和通信网络，长三角沿海地区可有效减少投资成本、避免闲置浪费。另外，通过联合开展技术攻关与试验验证，还可分摊研发投入并加速成果转化，显著降低整体创新成本。

二、推动长三角共建海上低空经济示范区的对策建议

（一）强化顶层设计与规划协同

一是建立长三角海上低空经济区域协同机制。建议由长三角区域合作办公室牵头，成立三省一市政府及民航华东管理局、交通运输部东海航海保障中心等参与的联合领导小组，建立季度联席会议制度，统筹空域审批、基建规划等核心事务。二是联合编制专项规划。在摸清空域资源、产业基础等海上低空经济发展“家底”的基础上，组织编制《长三角海上低空经济示范区建设方案》，明确上海临港“开放枢纽”、舟山“海岛样板”等分工清单，并与各自现行的低空经济规划做好衔接。三是推进适航审定创新。争取中国民航适航审定中心区域分中心落户上海，支持舟山试点海岛无人机适航快速认证，台州开展eVTOL海上环境适应性测试，形成符合长三角特色的适航标准体系。四是争创国家级试点。整合上海交大低空研究院、南航无人机研究院等资源，编制海上低空运行规范，推动上升为国家标准，同步申报国家级海上低空经济示范片区。

（二）联建基础设施与航线网络

一是构建空域协同管理体系。整合虹桥城际航线、沪舟甬物流航线等现有资源，绘制“长三角海上低空数字空域图”，划设沪甬跨湾、苏浙岛际等3类跨区域骨干航线。成立长三角海上低空空域管控中心，采用“分层分级+一线多用”模式，实现300米以下空域灵活调度。二是推动起降设施一体化。统一四地通航停机坪、海上浮台等设施共享标准，在上海洋山港、宁波北仑港、南通吕四港等枢纽布局“陆—港—海”联运起降场，同步推进启东江海澜湾通用机场与舟山海岛起降场联动。三是搭建空海通信网络。联合建设海上5G-A基站与卫星互联网融合系统，实现沿海300公里低空全覆盖，开发跨区域通信融合协议，共建长三角低空飞行服务与数据共享平台。

（三）推动海上低空产业链融合

一是共建产业协同创新平台。整合上海交大低空技术实验室、中科院宁波材料所、安徽零重力无人机实验室等机构，成立长三角海上低空联合创新中心。面向海上低空大跨度、强风扰、高盐雾等特点，聚力开展长续航、抗风扰、耐腐蚀的飞行设备研发创新，打造全国海上低空创新高地。二是打造产业分工生态。引导各地差异化布局海上低空经济产业环节，上海聚焦研发与适航审定、江苏主攻无人机整机制造、浙江发展运营服务、安徽强化零部件配套，形成“研发制造-测试验证-应用示范”一体化的产业生态。三是组建海上低空经济产业联盟。依托长三角低空经济产业联盟，设立海上低空经济发展子联盟，吸纳通航制造、海洋工程、数据服务、安全监管等上下游企业，形成信息互通与标准共建平台，打造创业孵化基地，加快技术联合研发和应用场景协同拓展。

（四）加快特色场景规模化应用

一是开展海上低空飞行场景需求调查。定期征集长三角地区政府部门、相关企业海上低空场景应用的空域和航线需求，形成需求指导目录与可行分析报告，为场景规模化应用提供数据支撑。二是发展海上物流“空海联运”。以港口、海岛等优势资源为节点，以沪舟甬航线为基础，统筹布设无人机起降点、数据通信基站等，积极推动船舶物资供给、海岛应急物资配送、高价值海鲜配送等场景应用，试点上海临港至舟山衢山岛的无人机物流专线。三推动陆海基建“空海运维”。开展无人机与运维船空海联合常态化巡检，用于港口、海上风电场、海洋牧场、海上通信基站等基础设施的日常维护和应急抢险，实施“空检+海修”联动作业。四是拓展海洋文旅“空海联游”。串联普陀山、东极岛、启东圆陀角等热门景点，试点开通跳岛低空旅游路线，推动低空经济与滨海旅游产业融合，在开阔海岸、海岛谋划低空海上飞行体验项目，依托休闲型海洋牧场探索“低空+海钓”休闲项目，打造长三角滨海文旅新IP。

The low-altitude economy is a hallmark of new-quality productive forces. It is defined by three-dimensional spatial use, deep industrial integration, and strong spillover effects. The ocean is one of its most promising arenas. From maritime logistics and ocean ranching to offshore energy operations and coastal tourism, low-altitude applications over the sea offer broad development potential.

The Yangtze River Delta (YRD) possesses a uniquely diverse mix of rivers, bays, islands, and open seas, giving it a natural advantage for developing a maritime low-altitude economy. All three provinces and one municipality in the region have already released dedicated plans for low-altitude economic development. Given the strong complementarity among coastal YRD cities in terms of resource endowments, infrastructure, and industrial foundations, there is both a basis and a necessity for joint action to build a shared maritime low-altitude economy demonstration zone. Doing so would help avoid duplicated investment and wasted resources, strengthen comparative advantages, and position the low-altitude economy as a key lever for advancing deeper YRD integration.

I. Why a Joint Maritime Low-Altitude Demonstration Zone Is Feasible

1. Strong alignment with national strategies
First, a joint initiative closely follows the national strategy for integrated development of the Yangtze River Delta. The Three-Year Action Plan for Yangtze River Delta Integration (2024–2026) explicitly calls for building a regional low-altitude infrastructure network. As a new carrier for cross-regional coordination, the low-altitude economy can link world-class port clusters such as Shanghai Port, Ningbo–Zhoushan Port, and Nantong Port, helping form a coastal economic belt with coordinated “land–sea–air” interaction.

Second, it aligns with the differentiated pilot positioning of coastal YRD cities as early movers in low-altitude development. Shanghai has proposed building a “sea–shore–city” smart logistics system; Nantong emphasizes low-altitude industries with river–sea characteristics; Ningbo aims to become a leading coastal and bay-oriented low-altitude ecosystem city; and Zhoushan seeks to create a model for island-based low-altitude development. While all focus on “low-altitude plus ocean” as a core application direction, each has distinct priorities. A jointly built demonstration zone would allow these differences to complement one another and collectively expand the overall scale of the low-altitude economy.

2. Favorable conditions for spatial and locational synergy
Geographic resources are highly complementary. Areas such as Shanghai Lingang and Qidong in Jiangsu sit at the confluence of rivers and seas, while Zhoushan and Ningbo in Zhejiang feature dense deep-water bays and island clusters. Dongtai in Jiangsu and Taizhou in Zhejiang offer expansive bay resources. Together, these diverse land–sea forms create a layered “river–bay–island–sea” spatial structure that supports a full range of low-altitude flight scenarios.

Airspace connectivity is also improving. Nationwide, less than 30 percent of low-altitude airspace is currently available for general aviation, and most of it remains fragmented. In contrast, parts of the YRD have already achieved contiguous low-altitude operations, such as the Shanghai–Zhoushan–Ningbo routes that cross provincial boundaries and cover island areas. Closer coordination among coastal cities would help upgrade airspace management from isolated openings to connected corridors.

Finally, there is strong land–sea industrial linkage. By leveraging major land transport arteries such as the Shanghai–Nanjing–Hangzhou expressways and coastal high-speed rail, and coordinating with the YRD’s world-class port clusters, the region can integrate general aviation airports in places like Qingpu (Shanghai), Deqing (Zhejiang), and Qidong (Jiangsu) with drone takeoff and landing sites in Zhoushan and Taizhou, forming a three-dimensional network that connects inland bases, port hubs, and offshore expansion areas.

3. Significant potential for economic coordination
Industrial chains across the region are highly complementary. Shanghai hosts around half of the country’s eVTOL innovation firms and can lead in R&D and standards setting. Jiangsu cities such as Nanjing’s Pukou District and Dongtai cluster more than 50 low-altitude manufacturing enterprises, with capabilities spanning complete drones and key components. Zhoushan offers the largest test flight airspace in East China. Together, coastal YRD cities can build a closed-loop industrial chain covering “R&D–manufacturing–test flights–operations.”

Application scenarios also overlap substantially. Coastal cities face similar needs in areas such as ocean ranch monitoring, offshore wind power maintenance, and island logistics. A jointly built low-altitude platform can be rolled out across multiple scenarios at once, enabling scaled operations and spreading operating costs.

Resource sharing can further reduce duplication. Ningbo has a first-mover advantage in general aviation airports, with projects already underway in Qianwan New Area and Ninghai. Zhoushan, meanwhile, has planned 23 drone takeoff and landing sites. Coordinated planning of airports, drone sites, and communications networks would lower investment costs and avoid idle capacity. Joint infrastructure planning and shared R&D efforts would also spread development costs, accelerate commercialization, and significantly reduce overall innovation expenses.

II. Policy Measures to Advance Joint Development

1. Strengthen top-level design and planning coordination
A regional coordination mechanism for the YRD maritime low-altitude economy should be established, led by the YRD Regional Cooperation Office and involving governments from the three provinces and Shanghai, along with relevant civil aviation and maritime authorities. A quarterly coordination meeting system could oversee key issues such as airspace approval and infrastructure planning.

A dedicated plan—the Yangtze River Delta Maritime Low-Altitude Economy Demonstration Zone Development Plan—should be jointly compiled after a thorough assessment of airspace resources and industrial foundations. This plan would clarify functional roles, such as Shanghai Lingang as an “open hub” and Zhoushan as an “island model,” while aligning with existing local low-altitude strategies.

Innovation in airworthiness certification should also be encouraged. Efforts could be made to attract a regional branch of the Civil Aviation Administration’s airworthiness certification center to Shanghai, support fast-track certification for island-based drones in Zhoushan, and carry out marine-environment adaptability tests for eVTOL aircraft in Taizhou, forming a YRD-specific certification system. At the same time, resources from leading universities and research institutes can be integrated to develop maritime low-altitude operation standards and seek national-level pilot designation.

2. Jointly build infrastructure and route networks
An integrated airspace management system should be developed by pooling existing routes such as intercity corridors around Hongqiao and logistics routes linking Shanghai, Zhoushan, and Ningbo. A digital “YRD maritime low-altitude airspace map” could define several cross-regional trunk routes, including cross-bay and inter-island corridors, supported by a regional airspace control center using layered and flexible management below 300 meters.

Takeoff and landing facilities should be integrated under unified standards for general aviation aprons and offshore platforms. Multimodal “land–port–sea” hubs can be developed at major ports such as Yangshan, Beilun, and Lüsi, while linking Qidong’s general aviation airport with island sites in Zhoushan.

A combined air–sea communications network should be built through joint deployment of offshore 5G-A base stations and satellite internet systems, achieving full low-altitude coverage along 300 kilometers of coastline and supporting a shared flight service and data platform.

3. Promote integration across the maritime low-altitude industrial chain
A collaborative innovation platform should be established by integrating key laboratories and institutes across the region. Focusing on challenges unique to maritime low-altitude operations—long distances, strong winds, and high salinity—joint R&D can target long-endurance, wind-resistant, and corrosion-resistant aircraft, positioning the YRD as a national innovation hub.

A clear division of labor should guide industrial layout: Shanghai concentrating on R&D and certification, Jiangsu on complete aircraft manufacturing, Zhejiang on operational services, and Anhui on component supply. This would create an integrated ecosystem spanning “R&D and manufacturing–testing and validation–application and demonstration.”

An industry alliance dedicated to the maritime low-altitude economy can bring together firms across general aviation manufacturing, marine engineering, data services, and safety regulation, supporting information sharing, joint standards, startup incubation, and coordinated expansion of application scenarios.

4. Accelerate large-scale application of signature scenarios
Regular surveys should be conducted to collect demand from governments and enterprises for maritime low-altitude routes and airspace, forming a guidance catalogue and feasibility analyses to support scaled deployment.

In logistics, “air–sea intermodal transport” should be promoted by using ports and islands as nodes, building on the Shanghai–Zhoushan–Ningbo routes, and deploying drone sites and communications infrastructure. Priority scenarios include ship supply delivery, emergency island logistics, and high-value seafood transport, with pilot routes such as Lingang–Qushan Island.

For infrastructure maintenance, routine joint inspections combining drones and service vessels can be used for ports, offshore wind farms, ocean ranches, and offshore communication facilities, enabling coordinated “air inspection plus sea repair.”

In tourism, low-altitude routes can link destinations such as Mount Putuo, Dongji Island, and Yuantuojiao in Qidong, supporting island-hopping sightseeing flights and immersive coastal flight experiences. Leisure-oriented ocean ranches can also explore “low-altitude plus sea fishing” activities, creating a new coastal tourism brand for the Yangtze River Delta.
