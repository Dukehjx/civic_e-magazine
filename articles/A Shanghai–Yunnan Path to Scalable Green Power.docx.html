A Shanghai–Yunnan Path to Scalable Green Power

沪滇新型储能产业双向赋能对策建议

随着“双碳”战略加速推进，传统能源与新能源正在“一出一进”。实现“双碳”目标，传统能源逐步退出必须建立在新能源安全可靠的替代基础之上，但目前新能源大规模利用仍面临间歇性、波动性、不稳定的挑战。作为破题之道，新型储能可以平抑波动，支撑光电风电大规模并网，被视为新型电力系统的重要技术和基础装备，实现“双碳”目标的重要支撑。同时，新型储能也是催生国内能源新业态、抢占国际战略新高地的重要领域。“风口”之下，各地产业布局也出现新动向，以储能技术为代表的万亿级绿色低碳新赛道逐渐展开。沪滇两地在推进新型储能产业发展方面大有可为，为打造未来新型储能产业创新高地，发展壮大未来新型储能产业集群，上海社会科学院课题组通过调研，梳理两地在新型储能产业发展方面的难度瓶颈，并提出进一步推动沪滇新型储能产业双向赋能的相关对策建议。

一、上海新型储能产业发展存在的难度瓶颈

1、绿电成本高。上海市内绿色能源资源匮乏，无水电以及抽水蓄能，核电厂资源缺乏，光伏发电因地域狭小可新增规模有限。上海作为一个相对缺乏新能源资源储备的城市，仍然需要通过引进大量外来电实现降碳的目标。新型储能产业是减碳的产业，但是这个产业产品的制备过程，无论电池生产及组装，还是正负极等关键材料的生产，都是一个高耗能的过程。绿色能源是产业的一个“隐形关键”。因此，绿电匮乏是限制上海新型储能产业发展的关键之一。相比较，云南水电、风电、光电等绿色能源占比近90%，全省电力市场化交易走在全国前列，工业到户电价年均价格低于全国平均水平。

2、上游原材料资源禀赋匮乏。新型储能产业链上游主要为锂、镍、铝、钢等金属原材料及构成锂电池的正负极材料、隔膜、电解液等。进一步可分成两个部分，一部分为镍钴锰矿、锂矿、铝矿、铁矿、石墨矿等原材料，另一部分为锂电池的关键组成部分，即正极材料、负极材料、隔膜及电解液，其中正极材料是锂电池的核心材料，是决定电池性能的关键因素。上述这些新型储能上游原材料在上海的禀赋均较为匮乏。相比较，云南拥有丰沃的磷、锂、锰、铜、镍、铝、石墨等新型储能电池主要材料资源储量，其中磷、锂、镍储量位居全国前3。

3、示范规模较小。上海已出台了氢能产业发展中长期规划，明确提出重点围绕交通和储能等氢能应用标准，加强相关标准体系研究，在能源领域，有序开展氢气储能等试点示范，开展氢储能在可再生能源消纳、电网调峰、绿色数据中心等场景的应用。目前已建立了一批示范先导区域和项目，发动了一批大型企业、机构参与建设。但氢能产业仍面临已有示范规模较小，氢能应用领域仍比较窄等问题。上海目前仅在工业、医疗、交通等领域的较小范围内适用氢能，且用量有限。氢燃料电池在重型车辆及船舶、航空等领域的应用，氢储能在可再生能源消纳、电网调峰、绿色数据中心等领域的应用，均有待推进和拓展。相比较，云南具备可再生能源消纳、电网调峰、绿色数据中心等多应用场景，有助于扩大示范规模，增加应用场景，实现降本增效。

二、云南新型储能产业发展存在的难度瓶颈

1、储能产业链条正处于培育阶段。目前，云南省储能产业发展主要集中在大理州和楚雄州。其中，大理州5个新能源装备制造项目已投产，包括6GWh全自动电化学储能集成工厂项目和4GWh锂电池储能系统项目；楚雄州的大姚县和姚安县正在打造储能产业园建设项目，大姚县聚焦储能产品研发、生产、供应、销售全链，姚安县重点建设储能系统装备制造、新型储能电池生产、集中式共享储能电站三个板块。虽然全省第一个锂电池储能装备制造项目远信年产2GWh锂电池储能装备制造项目已建成投产，但云南新型储能产业链条正处于培育阶段，缺少龙头企业和头部企业，技术路线较为单一，产业链各环节优势不明显。相比较，上海新型储能产业已形成了全产业链各个环节，多种技术路线，龙头集聚等优势。

2、技术路线面临替代风险。目前云南省锂电池材料产业重点企业采用的技术路线多数为目前国内外普遍采用的技术路线，但从技术发展趋势来看，面临着如何应对技术替代的风险。从全球趋势分析，锂固态电池是动力电池的发展方向，尽管目前成本较高，但一旦得到产业化应用，从其技术原理来看，隔膜和电解液就不再必需，云南省目前大力引进的石墨负极材料技术路线也将被替代，磷酸铁锂正极材料的技术路线也可能将面临未知的影响。另外经济性驱动钠离子电池产业化也不断加速，钠离子电池产业链目前开始形成。云南省锂电池材料产业届时将面临较大的产业发展风险。相比较，钠离子电池应用示范项目已在上海超碳中心落地，成为上海储能新产业，且上海已拥有钠创新能源、派能科技、贲安能源、璞钠能源等多家龙头企业，集聚效应显著。

3、核心关键技术、人才缺乏。云南省在锂离子电池隔膜方面产能和技术方面优势在全国领先，在全球具有一定的影响力，但在正极、负极、电解液等关键环节缺乏自主核心技术。虽然云南省在锂产业及新能源领域已建成一批重点实验室和研发平台，但对产业技术创新的支撑仍显不足。此外，专业储能人才较为匮乏。储能人才需要跨界掌握电化学、电力电子、材料科学、机械工程等学科的知识，尤其在储能生态链中，核心材料技术亟待突破。相比较，上海在绿色低碳领域拥有众多人才、世界一流大学、高水平研发机构和行业领军企业等，具备产业、人才、教育、创新“四链融合”。

三、进一步推动沪滇新型储能产业双向赋能的对策建议

1、推动新型储能产业形成“上海研发+云南制造”双向赋能

支持上海设立专门的新型储能技术研发基地，提供现代化的实验室和研发设施，吸引云南储能产业链上企业入驻，共同研发，共享创新。组织沪滇两地新型储能装备制造企业、储能电池企业、科研机构、检测认证机构、高等院校等建立新型储能产业联盟，推动上下游企业对接融通，强化两地行业、组织交流，通过协同技术、装备、人才、资金等各类资源，推动沪滇新型储能产业链协同发展。依托沪滇协作资源和力量，聚焦新型储能产业园区建设，发展“飞地经济”，成立“沪滇合作共建高质量发展‘飞地经济’新型储能产业园区工作专班”，探索通过“飞地经济”模式，以共建新型储能产业园区为载体，更好地把上海的资金、技术、人才等优势与云南的资源、人力、生态等优势有机结合起来，有效促进要素流动、产业互动、园区联动，实现两地互利共赢、共同发展。

2、推动新型储能产业形成“上海市场+云南资源”双向赋能

推动沪滇两地政府、企业签订深化战略合作框架协议，建立战略合作伙伴关系，推进云南绿色能源装备制造产业投资、清洁能源开发以及新能源+产业链等方面开展深度合作，推动云南绿色能源发展与绿色装备制造业深度融合，规划投资建设风、光、储一体化的零碳工业园示范项目，打造云南“绿色能源牌”品牌。推动上海电力等上海企业与云南电力等云南企业深度合作，促进云南可再生能源富集区域的新型电力系统建设，实施一批新型电力系统建设示范工程，积极探索云电入沪，组织绿电跨省交易，实现上海绿电来源覆盖云南，打造以“规范化标准、协同化响应、多元化体验”为特征的“一站式”沪滇绿电交易服务体系，实现云南绿电资源入沪，赋能上海市场。

3、推动新型储能产业形成“上海技术+云南场景”双向赋能

推进上海氢储能在云南可再生能源消纳、电网调峰、绿色数据中心、交通运输等领域的应用，探索培育“风光发电+氢储能”一体化应用新模式，实现云南场景拓展上海储能技术示范规模与范围。充分利用“1+16+n”沪滇产业园区合作体系，鼓励钠创新能源、派能科技、贲安能源、璞钠能源等上海钠离子电池龙头企业落户云南，形成“上海总部+云南基地”的模式，实现上海钠离子电池技术应用场景拓展，同时赋能云南储能技术路线摆脱被替代风险。充分发挥上海已有氢能产业技术优势，探索沪滇氢能产业协同发展的商业化路径，以“上海技术+云南场景”双向赋能来加快推动上海氢能产业发展和云南氢能制储输运销用全链条发展。

As China’s “dual carbon” strategy accelerates, traditional energy sources are gradually exiting while new energy sources scale up—one moving out, the other moving in. Achieving the dual carbon goals requires that the phase-out of traditional energy be firmly grounded in the safe and reliable substitution of renewables. Yet large-scale deployment of renewable energy still faces challenges of intermittency, volatility, and instability.

New-type energy storage offers a way forward. By smoothing fluctuations and supporting the large-scale grid integration of solar and wind power, it is widely regarded as a core technology and foundational equipment of the new power system, as well as a critical pillar for achieving the dual carbon targets. At the same time, new-type energy storage is a strategic frontier for fostering new domestic energy business models and securing a competitive edge internationally.

Amid this surge of momentum, local industrial布局 is shifting rapidly. Green, low-carbon sectors—represented by energy storage technologies and measured in the trillions of yuan—are opening up as new growth tracks. Shanghai and Yunnan are well positioned to work together in advancing the new-type energy storage industry. With the aim of building a future innovation highland and scaling up a competitive industrial cluster, a research team from the Shanghai Academy of Social Sciences conducted field studies to identify key bottlenecks in both regions and propose policy measures to further promote two-way empowerment between Shanghai and Yunnan in new-type energy storage.

I. Bottlenecks in the Development of Shanghai’s New-Type Energy Storage Industry

1. High costs of green electricity
Shanghai lacks local green energy resources. It has no hydropower or pumped-storage facilities, limited nuclear power resources, and only modest room for expanding photovoltaic generation due to land constraints. As a city with relatively scarce renewable energy reserves, Shanghai still relies heavily on importing electricity from other regions to reduce carbon emissions.

Although new-type energy storage is itself a low-carbon industry, the production process—whether battery manufacturing and assembly or the production of key materials such as cathodes and anodes—is highly energy-intensive. Green electricity therefore becomes an “invisible but critical” input. Scarcity of green power has emerged as a key constraint on Shanghai’s energy storage industry.

By contrast, nearly 90 percent of Yunnan’s power mix comes from hydropower, wind, and solar. The province leads nationally in electricity market-based trading, and its average industrial electricity prices are consistently below the national average.

2. Limited endowment of upstream raw materials
Upstream segments of the new-type energy storage value chain rely on metals such as lithium, nickel, aluminum, and steel, as well as key battery materials including cathodes, anodes, separators, and electrolytes. These inputs can be divided into two categories: mineral resources—such as nickel–cobalt–manganese ores, lithium ore, aluminum ore, iron ore, and graphite—and the core components of lithium batteries, with cathode materials being the most decisive factor for battery performance.

Shanghai is poorly endowed with nearly all of these upstream resources. Yunnan, in contrast, holds abundant reserves of phosphorus, lithium, manganese, copper, nickel, aluminum, and graphite. Its reserves of phosphorus, lithium, and nickel rank among the top three nationwide.

3. Limited scale of demonstration projects
Shanghai has issued a medium- and long-term plan for hydrogen energy development, emphasizing standards for hydrogen applications in transport and energy storage. In the energy sector, it has launched pilot projects for hydrogen energy storage and explored applications in renewable energy integration, grid peak shaving, and green data centers. A number of pilot zones and projects are already in place, with participation from major enterprises and institutions.

However, the hydrogen energy industry still faces small demonstration scales and narrow application scopes. Current use is largely limited to small-scale applications in industry, healthcare, and transport. Broader deployment—such as hydrogen fuel cells for heavy-duty vehicles, ships, and aviation, or hydrogen storage for renewable integration, grid balancing, and green data centers—has yet to gain momentum.

Yunnan, by comparison, offers a wider range of application scenarios, including renewable energy integration, grid peak regulation, and green data centers. These conditions are conducive to scaling up demonstrations, expanding use cases, and reducing costs.

II. Bottlenecks in the Development of Yunnan’s New-Type Energy Storage Industry

1. An industry chain still in the cultivation stage
Yunnan’s energy storage industry is currently concentrated in Dali Prefecture and Chuxiong Prefecture. In Dali, five new energy equipment manufacturing projects are already operational, including a fully automated 6 GWh electrochemical energy storage integration plant and a 4 GWh lithium battery storage system project. In Chuxiong, Dayao County and Yao’an County are developing energy storage industrial parks, with Dayao focusing on the full chain from R&D and production to supply and sales, and Yao’an prioritizing system equipment manufacturing, new-type storage battery production, and centralized shared storage stations.

Although Yunnan’s first lithium battery storage equipment project—Yuanxin’s 2 GWh annual-capacity plant—has entered operation, the overall industry chain remains in its early stages. Leading and flagship enterprises are scarce, technology routes are relatively concentrated, and advantages across different segments are not yet clear. Shanghai, in contrast, has already formed a full-chain ecosystem with multiple technology pathways and a strong concentration of leading firms.

2. Exposure to technology substitution risks
Most leading lithium battery material producers in Yunnan adopt mainstream technology routes currently used domestically and internationally. However, longer-term trends point to significant substitution risks. Globally, solid-state lithium batteries are widely seen as the future direction for power batteries. While costs remain high, large-scale commercialization would eliminate the need for separators and electrolytes, potentially displacing Yunnan’s heavily promoted graphite anode routes and affecting lithium iron phosphate cathode technologies in unpredictable ways.

At the same time, cost-driven advances are accelerating the industrialization of sodium-ion batteries, whose industry chain is now taking shape. These trends pose notable risks for Yunnan’s lithium battery materials sector. Shanghai, by contrast, has already launched sodium-ion battery demonstration projects at the Shanghai Super Carbon Center and hosts leading firms such as Natrium Innovation Energy, Pylon Technologies, Benan Energy, and Puna Energy, forming a strong cluster effect.

3. Shortages of core technologies and specialized talent
Yunnan holds national leadership and global influence in lithium-ion battery separator capacity and technology, but lacks proprietary core technologies in cathodes, anodes, and electrolytes. While the province has established several key laboratories and R&D platforms in lithium and new energy fields, their support for industrial innovation remains insufficient.

In addition, there is a shortage of specialized energy storage talent. The field requires interdisciplinary expertise spanning electrochemistry, power electronics, materials science, and mechanical engineering, with particular urgency in core materials technologies. Shanghai, by comparison, benefits from abundant green and low-carbon talent, world-class universities, high-level research institutions, and industry leaders—enabling deep integration across industry, talent, education, and innovation.

III. Policy Recommendations for Deepening Shanghai–Yunnan Two-Way Empowerment

1. Build a “Shanghai R&D + Yunnan Manufacturing” model
Support the establishment of dedicated new-type energy storage R&D bases in Shanghai, equipped with advanced laboratories and facilities, and encourage Yunnan-based firms along the storage value chain to co-locate for joint research and shared innovation.

Organize energy storage equipment manufacturers, battery firms, research institutes, testing and certification bodies, and universities from both regions into an industry alliance. Through coordinated use of technology, equipment, talent, and capital, promote integrated development of the Shanghai–Yunnan energy storage value chain.

Leveraging existing cooperation mechanisms, focus on building joint industrial parks through an “enclave economy” model. By combining Shanghai’s strengths in capital, technology, and talent with Yunnan’s advantages in resources, labor, and ecology, this approach can enhance factor mobility, industrial interaction, and park-level coordination, delivering mutual benefit and shared growth.

2. Build a “Shanghai Market + Yunnan Resources” model
Encourage governments and enterprises in both regions to sign strategic cooperation frameworks, deepening collaboration in green energy equipment manufacturing, clean energy development, and new energy–industry chain integration. Plan and invest in integrated wind–solar–storage zero-carbon industrial park demonstration projects, strengthening Yunnan’s “green energy” brand.

Promote deeper cooperation between companies such as Shanghai Electric Power and Yunnan Energy Investment Group to advance new-type power system construction in renewable-rich areas. Explore large-scale projects and actively pilot “Yunnan power to Shanghai,” enabling cross-provincial green electricity trading. Build a one-stop Shanghai–Yunnan green power trading service system characterized by standardized rules, coordinated response, and diversified user experiences, allowing Yunnan’s green power resources to directly empower Shanghai’s market.

3. Build a “Shanghai Technology + Yunnan Scenarios” model
Expand the application of Shanghai’s hydrogen storage technologies in Yunnan across renewable integration, grid peak regulation, green data centers, and transport, exploring integrated “wind–solar generation + hydrogen storage” models and scaling up demonstration effects.

Make full use of the “1+16+n” Shanghai–Yunnan industrial park cooperation system to encourage leading Shanghai sodium-ion battery firms—such as Natrium Innovation Energy, Pylon Technologies, Benan Energy, and Puna Energy—to establish operations in Yunnan under a “Shanghai headquarters + Yunnan base” structure. This would both expand application scenarios for Shanghai’s technologies and help Yunnan mitigate substitution risks.

Finally, leverage Shanghai’s existing strengths in hydrogen technologies to explore commercially viable pathways for coordinated hydrogen industry development, accelerating Shanghai’s hydrogen sector while advancing Yunnan’s full-chain capabilities in hydrogen production, storage, transport, and utilization through two-way empowerment.
