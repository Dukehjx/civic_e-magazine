The low-altitude economy offers new pathways for regional development, yet cross-regional coordination challenges persist. The Yangtze River Delta can learn from international examples to establish joint mechanisms, improve airspace management, and build a shared governance framework.

I. Why Low-Altitude Matters

The low-altitude economy covers activities below 1,000 meters involving drones, eVTOLs, general aviation, and related services. Globally, this sector has grown rapidly, supporting logistics, emergency response, tourism, and urban mobility.

China has issued policies to accelerate low-altitude economic development, with local governments launching demonstration zones and industrial clusters. The Yangtze River Delta, with its manufacturing, technology, and logistics strengths, is well-positioned to lead.

II. Challenges in the Yangtze River Delta

1. Fragmented Airspace Management

Low-altitude airspace management remains divided across administrative boundaries. Shanghai, Jiangsu, Zhejiang, and Anhui apply different rules, restricting cross-regional flight approvals and operations. This fragmentation raises costs, complicates logistics, and slows commercial adoption.

2. Incompatible Infrastructure

Each province builds vertiports, charging stations, and communication networks independently, resulting in incompatible standards. Without shared infrastructure, regional integration becomes difficult, limiting operational efficiency.

3. Data Silos and Safety Gaps

Flight control data, weather monitoring, and safety protocols are not shared across regions. This creates safety risks, complicates emergency coordination, and reduces transparency for regulators and operators.

4. Uncoordinated Industrial Policies

Shanghai, Jiangsu, Zhejiang, and Anhui each promote low-altitude industries through independent subsidies, tax incentives, and certification programs. This leads to redundant investments, policy conflicts, and unfair competition.

III. International Examples

1. European Union: U-Space Framework

The EU's U-Space system integrates drone traffic management across member states, providing shared airspace data, standardized flight rules, and automated coordination. This approach enables cross-border operations while maintaining safety and transparency.

2. United States: NASA UTM Program

NASA's Unmanned Traffic Management (UTM) system enables safe drone operations through real-time data sharing, automated flight planning, and coordination with traditional air traffic control. It has been tested in partnership with state and local governments.

3. Japan: Sky Highway Concept

Japan is building dedicated "sky highways" for drones, separating commercial traffic from restricted airspace. Regional governments cooperate to align standards, safety protocols, and operational permissions.

IV. Recommendations for the Yangtze River Delta

1. Establish a Regional Coordination Office

Create a joint Yangtze River Delta Low-Altitude Coordination Office responsible for harmonizing airspace policies, approving cross-regional operations, and mediating conflicts. This office should include representatives from civil aviation authorities, local governments, and industry.

2. Build Shared Infrastructure

Develop unified standards for vertiports, charging networks, and communication systems across Shanghai, Jiangsu, Zhejiang, and Anhui. Use joint investments to build shared facilities, reducing costs and improving compatibility.

3. Create a Regional Data Platform

Establish a Yangtze River Delta Low-Altitude Data Platform to share real-time flight data, weather conditions, restricted zones, and safety alerts. This platform should integrate with national air traffic systems while remaining accessible to local operators.

4. Align Industrial Policies

Coordinate subsidies, certification standards, and support programs to avoid duplication. Encourage regional specialization鈥擲hanghai can focus on eVTOL R&D, Jiangsu on manufacturing, Zhejiang on logistics, and Anhui on testing and demonstration.

5. Launch Cross-Regional Pilot Projects

Test coordinated low-altitude operations through pilot projects, such as cross-provincial medical supply delivery or tourist routes spanning multiple cities. Use lessons from these projects to refine policies and expand regional integration.
