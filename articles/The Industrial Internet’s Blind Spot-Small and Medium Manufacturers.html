The Industrial Internet’s Blind Spot: Small and Medium Manufacturers

强化工业互联网平台赋能效应

推动上海中小企业加快数字化转型的政策建议

推动工业领域中小企业加快数字化转型，是推动新型工业化的重要任务之一，也是践行吉宁书记“在稳定工业大盘中坚定转型升级”工作要求的关键举措。本专报重点梳理总结上海中小企业数字化转型的瓶颈障碍，从打造精准政策支持体系、搭建供需对接渠道平台、强化中小企业转型积极性的多重视角，提出上海在强化工业互联网平台赋能效应，推动上海中小企业加快数字化转型的政策建议。

一、主要问题

1、政策端偏重“抓大放小”，工业互联网平台策源端政策支持缺失

工业互联网平台企业按照成长脉络可以分类两类：第一类是从超大型制造或互联网企业业务链条中衍生而来，如卡奥斯、宝信软件、犀牛工厂等，该类平台更多立足所属行业开展上下游供应链优化整合，但无法深入到企业生产技术环节对其进行数字化改造提升；第二类平台则由专注于工业生产细分环节的工业服务企业平台化转型升级而来。目前上海严重缺乏具有生态主导力和创新引领力的第二类平台，且该类平台的培育策源必须以强大的工业服务业集群为基础，还应具备在中小型工业服务企业当中发现、挖掘与支持“独角兽”企业与“瞪羚”企业的政策支持体系。然而，上海在近期出台《关于促进本市生产性互联网服务平台高质量发展的若干意见》和《上海市促进工业服务业赋能产业升级行动方案（2024-2027年）》当中，首批重点培育的67家重点平台遴选仍然“抓大放小”，以龙头型和高成长型平台为主。当前上海政府主管部门缺乏对工业服务业未来平台化转型升级趋势的认知，因此没有针对工业服务企业当中的“独角兽”企业和“瞪羚”企业制定更为精准的优质企业挖掘发现机制与专项支持政策。

2、供给端偏重“各自为战”，数字化转型服务供需对接平台缺失

部分上海工业服务企业向课题组反映，解决中小企业数字化转型痛点、堵点，往往需要将多个工业服务企业的产品和服务打包，才能形成一个完整有效的解决方案。但是，由于上海缺乏为中小企业数字化转型供需对接的统一服务平台，导致上海工业服务与聚焦于专业服务领域的工业互联网平台企业只能以“各自为战”方式为企业提供单点式服务，这一方面降低了工业服务业赋能产业升级的实际效能，也降低了中小企业在数字化转型过程中的获得感和满意度。与之形成鲜明对比的是，位于济南的浪潮云洲工业互联网依托国家级中小企业数字化转型促进中心这一公共服务平台，通过为中小企业提供数转诊断供需对接、完整解决方案提供等服务，集聚供需双侧资源，全方位打通中小企业数字化转型供需对接通道。自2024年5月成立至今，该平台已经为山东超过4000家中小企业提供数转解决方案并推动其转型方案落地实施。

3、需求端存在“认知偏差”，数字化转型自主积极性严重不足

课题组调研发现，低投入、高产出、快部署、提质降本增效是广大制造业中小企业最迫切需求。然而，部分工业服务企业和工业互联网平台企业负责人向课题组反映，广大中小企业因为对数字化转型后的降本增效效果存疑，导致其对数字化转型普遍存在“不想转、不会转、不敢转”的认知偏差。“不想转”和“不敢转”主要是中小企业负责人对数字化转型的实际投入产出比存在较大顾虑，“不会转”体现在企业难以通过自身资源、信息和渠道，顺利获取符合企业转型需求的服务和产品。综上，中小企业数字化转型在认知层面存在较高“教育成本”，而目前这部分成本主要由工业互联网平台企业和工业服务企业承担，政府端、政策端缺位严重。

二、政策建议

1、优政策：聚焦“抓早抓小”，强化工业互联网平台策源端支持

建议上海改变过去“抓大放小”的政策支持思路。一是建立潜力型工业服务企业遴选与支持机制。由经信委和商务委等主管部门联合相关智库机构，加快制定潜力型工业服务企业遴选标准，挖掘其中的“独角兽”企业与“瞪羚”企业，并配套专项支持政策。二是建立工业服务企业平台化跃升支持机制。动态跟踪本市工业服务企业发展情况，针对发展至一定规模，有必要由传统工业服务企业转型跃升为工业互联网平台的“瞪羚”企业，从融资支持、转型诊断服务、人才培育、技术支持等领域制定精准灵活的政策支持体系，助力本市优质工业服务企业实现平台化升级。三是建立工业服务特色集聚园区，优化工业服务企业创新孵化服务体系。加快引进具有工业服务业特色的优质孵化器企业，谋划设立工业服务业特色集聚园区，从源头优化对工业服务业的孵化和培育，强化工业互联网产业创新策源功能，加快推动上海实现“工业服务业孵化培育——工业服务业平台化跃升——工业互联网平台集聚发展”的完整产业生态。

2、搭平台：打通供需对接渠道，提升中小企业数字化转型质效

一是在线上搭建具有上海特色的中小企业数字化转型对接服务平台。建议借鉴浪潮牵头建设“中小企业数字化转型促进中心”实践经验，引入浪潮云洲等国内头部工业互联网平台企业，或者遴选上海本地工业互联网平台企业，加快出台专项政策，支持其牵头搭建中小企业数字化转型对接服务平台，加快构建集中小企业数字化转型案例数据库、数据要素赋能平台、数字化转型政策服务、高标准数字化转型培训服务等为一体的公共服务平台。同步出台配套支持政策，不断优化平台运营实效。二是在线下搭建中小企业数字化转型创新体验中心。建议中小企业需求视角建设线下数字化转型服务体验中心，该中心应当整合既有中小企业数字化转型成功案例、服务商产品和服务资源、力争能够使中小企业既能“看服务”，又能“用服务”，为中小企业提供个性化的精准数转堵点诊断，打造“小快轻准”产品方案供给资源池，实现企业需求与解决方案自动精准匹配，形成“平台+服务+标准”模式，打造中小企业数字化转型公共服务平台全国标杆。

3、强激励：加快平台实体双向转型，畅通数实融合“最先一公里”

一是引导平台企业沿产业链供应链延伸赋能服务领域。鼓励引导上海工业互联网平台企业由单一生产环节数字化改造业务向为企业提供数字化营销及订单牵引的全产业链协同和全价值场景应用转型、延伸，强化数字化转型服务能力和扩展业务范围。二是优化前端政策设计，撬动中小企业数字化转型积极性。建议加快建立上海中小企业企业数字化转型探索年度白名单制度，对在政府引导下首次运用工业互联网平台尝试数字化转型而入选白名单的传统中小制造企业，配套相应财政资金支持政策，以政府购买方式为中小企业提供数字化转型痛点堵点诊断服务等，有效降低中小企业首次开展数字化转型的资金成本和风险，提升中小企业开展数字化转型的积极性。

Accelerating digital transformation in small and medium-sized industrial enterprises is a core task in advancing new-style industrialization. It is also central to the directive to “stabilize the industrial base while firmly pushing forward transformation and upgrading.” This policy brief reviews the key bottlenecks facing Shanghai’s SMEs in their digital transition and, from the perspectives of targeted policy design, supply–demand matching, and incentive mechanisms, puts forward concrete recommendations on how Shanghai can better leverage industrial internet platforms to empower SMEs and speed up their digital transformation.

I. Key Challenges

1. Policy focus remains tilted toward large players, leaving gaps at the source of platform innovation

Industrial internet platforms in China generally fall into two categories. The first consists of platforms spun off from large manufacturing conglomerates or internet giants, such as those originating within major industrial groups. These platforms tend to focus on optimizing supply chains within their own industries, but often struggle to penetrate deeply into firms’ core production processes to drive meaningful digital upgrades.

The second category grows out of specialized industrial service firms that evolve into platform-based providers by focusing on specific production segments. Shanghai currently lacks enough platforms of this second type with strong ecosystem leadership and innovation capacity. Cultivating them requires a robust industrial services cluster, as well as policies that can identify and support emerging “gazelle” and “unicorn” firms among smaller industrial service providers.

Recent policy documents, including initiatives to promote high-quality development of production-oriented internet service platforms and action plans for upgrading the industrial services sector, still lean heavily toward established or already high-growth platforms. Early-stage, smaller but high-potential firms receive far less attention. This reflects a broader gap in policy awareness of how industrial service firms are likely to evolve into platforms in the future, and it has resulted in a lack of tailored mechanisms to identify, nurture, and support next-generation platform leaders.

2. Fragmented supply-side efforts and the absence of a unified service-matching platform

Interviews with industrial service providers reveal that addressing SMEs’ digital transformation pain points often requires bundling solutions from multiple firms into an integrated package. Yet Shanghai lacks a unified platform to match supply and demand for SME digital services. As a result, industrial service firms and specialized industrial internet platforms tend to operate in isolation, offering piecemeal solutions.

This fragmentation weakens the overall effectiveness of digital empowerment and reduces SMEs’ sense of value and satisfaction during the transformation process. By contrast, practices elsewhere show what is possible. In Jinan, for example, an industrial internet platform backed by a national SME digital transformation promotion center provides diagnostics, matchmaking, and end-to-end solutions through a public service platform. Since its launch in mid-2024, it has already supported digital transformation for thousands of SMEs across the province, with solutions moving from design to implementation.

3. Demand-side perception gaps and weak intrinsic motivation among SMEs

Field research indicates that SMEs most urgently seek solutions that are low-cost, high-return, quick to deploy, and effective in improving quality while reducing costs. Yet many SME owners remain skeptical about whether digital transformation can truly deliver these benefits. This has led to a widespread mindset of “not wanting to transform, not knowing how to transform, and not daring to transform.”

Concerns about uncertain returns drive hesitation, while limited access to information, resources, and suitable service providers makes it difficult for firms to act even when they are willing. As a result, digital transformation among SMEs carries a high “education cost.” At present, this cost is borne largely by platform firms and service providers, while government involvement and policy support at this stage remain insufficient.

II. Policy Recommendations

1. Refine policy design by shifting from “supporting the big” to “supporting the early and the small”

Shanghai should move beyond a policy approach that prioritizes only large or mature players. First, it should establish clear criteria and mechanisms to identify high-potential industrial service firms, working jointly across relevant government departments and research institutions to spot emerging gazelle and unicorn companies and provide them with targeted support.

Second, a dedicated support framework should be created to help promising industrial service firms make the leap to platform-based models. This would include flexible, well-targeted policies covering financing, transformation diagnostics, talent development, and technical support, tailored to firms reaching the scale where platformization becomes viable.

Third, Shanghai should develop specialized industrial service clusters and incubation zones. By introducing high-quality incubators with a focus on industrial services, the city can strengthen early-stage nurturing and innovation, forming a complete ecosystem that links incubation, platform upgrading, and the clustered development of industrial internet platforms.

2. Build integrated platforms to connect supply and demand and improve transformation outcomes

At the online level, Shanghai should develop a city-specific digital transformation service platform for SMEs. Drawing on successful national practices, the city can invite leading industrial internet firms or select strong local platforms to take the lead, supported by dedicated policies. Such a platform should integrate case databases, data-enabled services, policy guidance, and standardized training into a single public service hub, with ongoing policy support to ensure effective operation.

Offline, Shanghai should establish digital transformation innovation and experience centers geared toward SME needs. These centers would combine real-world success cases, service-provider resources, and hands-on applications, allowing firms to both see and use digital solutions. By offering tailored diagnostics and a pool of lightweight, fast, and precise solution packages, these centers could enable automatic matching between enterprise needs and solutions, creating a replicable “platform + services + standards” model.

3. Strengthen incentives to unlock the “first mile” of digital–industrial integration

Platform firms should be encouraged to extend their services along industrial and supply chains, moving beyond single-process digitization toward integrated solutions that cover digital marketing, order matching, and full-value-chain coordination. This would expand the scope and impact of digital empowerment.

On the demand side, policy design should focus on lowering entry barriers for SMEs. One option is to introduce an annual “exploration whitelist” for SMEs undertaking digital transformation for the first time under government guidance. Firms on the list could receive financial support and government-purchased diagnostic services, reducing both the cost and risk of initial experimentation. By easing the first step, such measures can significantly boost SMEs’ willingness to engage in digital transformation and help bridge the gap between digital technologies and the real economy.
